Brief Dram Shop and the Sale-to-Visibly-Intoxicated Rule

Off-premises retailers face dram shop exposure too. Here's the short version.

What you'll learn

  • K8: MCA 27-1-710 applied to off-premise sales
  • P4: Refuse the sale to a visibly intoxicated person under either of Montana's two parallel intoxication-refusal standards (MCA 16-3-301(4)(b) three-prong; MCA 16-6-304 broader single-prong)
Lesson

Bars and restaurants think of dram shop as their problem. It isn't only theirs. If you sell beer or wine to an obviously drunk customer who then drives off and crashes, the off-premise retailer can be sued under MCA 27-1-710. The same three triggers that apply to a bar apply to your register.

Learning List

MCA 27-1-710 three triggers

  • Sale to underage without reasonable age verification
  • Sale to a visibly intoxicated person
  • Forced or coerced consumption / fraud about alcohol content
Note

Off-premise example. A clearly drunk customer comes into a c-store at 11 p.m., buys a 12-pack of beer, drives off, and crashes a quarter mile away. The injured driver's family can sue the c-store under MCA 27-1-710 if they can show the cashier knew or should have known the customer was visibly intoxicated. "He was just buying it for later" is not a defense if the cues were stacked at the register.

Note

The damage caps are the same as on-premise. • $250K cap on noneconomic damages per event (pain, suffering, loss of consortium) • $250K cap on punitive damages per event • Economic damages (medical, lost wages) uncapped • Plaintiff must serve certified-mail notice within 180 days of the sale or service; missing the 180-day window forfeits the dram-shop claim • 2-year statute of limitations for the civil action itself A store's exposure on a serious crash easily exceeds the caps because the medical damages are uncapped.

Lesson

If a customer in line is showing intoxication cues, refuse the sale. Document the refusal: time, customer description, cues you observed, what you said. The five seconds it takes you to refuse can be the difference between a regular Tuesday and significant dram-shop exposure on your store.

Note

Refusal scripts that work at the register: • "I can't sell this tonight, sorry. The law's on me, not on you." • "Tonight isn't going to work for the alcohol - coffee and the snack aisle are open." • "I want to keep my license. I can ring up everything else, just not this." Don't debate.

Don't justify. If they push, call the manager. The phrase "the law's on me" is the magic phrase: it shifts the refusal from you-versus-them to a legal floor that's not your call to make.

Scenario Practice

Scenario. A customer comes to your register with a 12-pack at 10:30 p.m. He smells strongly of alcohol, is swaying, and slurs slightly when answering "card or cash?" His friend who came in with him is sober and silent.
