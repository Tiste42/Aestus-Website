Alcohol and the Body

BAC, absorption, standard drinks, and how Montana defines an alcoholic beverage

What you'll learn

  • K2: Effects of alcohol on the body
  • K3: Standard drink equivalency and Montana's >0.5% ABV definition
  • P1: Translate menu items into standard-drink equivalents in real time
Lesson

Montana defines an alcoholic beverage as any beverage with more than 0.5% alcohol by volume (MCA 16-1-106(4)). That includes beer, wine, spirits, cider, hard seltzer, ready-to-drink cocktails, and pre-mixed canned drinks. Anything stronger than non-alcoholic beer falls under Montana's alcohol laws and triggers every rule in this course: ID checks, refusal-of-service obligations, dram shop liability under MCA 27-1-710, and the hours-of-service rules in MCA 16-3-304.

Law

MCA 16-1-106(4) (verbatim): "'Alcoholic beverage' means a compound produced and sold for human consumption as a drink that contains more than 0.5% of alcohol by volume."

Note

The 0.5% line at the bar. A few common items sit close to the threshold. Most kombucha is 0.5–2.5% ABV; the high end is alcohol under Montana law. "Non-alcoholic" beer is legally capped at 0.5% ABV, so it falls outside the rules, but a so-called "NA" craft option that prints 0.6% is in. Hard kombucha (3–9%) is fully alcoholic.

When a customer says "it's just a kombucha," check the label.

Lesson

Servers and bartenders need to think in standard drink equivalents because that is the unit alcohol affects the body in. The CDC and NIAAA define one standard drink as approximately 14 grams (0.6 fluid ounces) of pure ethanol. That works out to: 12 oz of beer at 5% ABV, 5 oz of wine at 12% ABV, or 1.5 oz of distilled spirits at 40% ABV (80 proof).

All three put the same amount of alcohol into the bloodstream.

Learning List

Real bar pours: how many standard drinks

  • 12 oz domestic beer at 5% ABV (Coors Banquet, Bud Light, Miller Lite) = 1.0 standard drink.
  • 12 oz craft lager at 6.5% ABV = 1.3 standard drinks.
  • 16 oz craft IPA at 7.5% ABV = 1.9 standard drinks.
  • 16 oz strong craft beer at 9% ABV = 2.3 standard drinks.
  • 5 oz wine pour at 12% ABV = 1.0. A restaurant 6 oz pour at 14% ABV (most California reds) = 1.4.
  • 1.5 oz spirit pour at 40% ABV (one shot) = 1.0. A 2 oz "tall" = 1.3. A 3 oz double = 2.0.
  • 12 oz hard seltzer at 5% ABV = 1.0. A 9% ABV version = 1.8.
  • Margarita with 2 oz tequila + 1 oz triple sec (typical bar pour) = ~1.7 standard drinks. A "long pour" version with 3 oz tequila = ~2.3.
Note

The pitfall. Customers count drinks. The body counts ounces of pure alcohol. A customer who tells you "I've only had two" but those two were 16 oz strong craft beers at 9% ABV has had the equivalent of almost 5 standard drinks.

The lesson: in your head, translate every order to standard drinks before you decide whether to pour the next one.

Lesson

Alcohol is absorbed mostly through the small intestine. About 20% absorbs through the stomach lining; the rest moves into the small intestine and crosses into the bloodstream. Once in the blood it reaches every organ within minutes-brain, heart, liver, kidneys.

The brain is the one that matters for service decisions: alcohol depresses central nervous system function, which is why coordination, judgment, and reaction time deteriorate before slurred speech and stumbling become visible.

Note

The 20–40 minute window. Alcohol takes 20 to 40 minutes to fully absorb after a drink. So a customer who pounded three shots at 9:00 PM may not show full impairment until 9:30 PM, when their BAC is still climbing. The most dangerous moments often come AFTER you've stopped serving someone-the alcohol they already drank is still hitting them. This is why "one for the road" is the worst possible service decision: you are pushing their final BAC even higher when it's already rising on its own.

Learning List

Factors that change how fast alcohol affects someone

  • Body weight: smaller people reach a given BAC on fewer drinks. A 110 lb adult reaches 0.08 BAC on roughly 2 drinks in an hour; a 220 lb adult needs about 4.
  • Sex: at the same weight, women typically reach a higher BAC than men because of body composition (more body fat, less water to dilute alcohol) and lower stomach alcohol-dehydrogenase activity.
  • Food: drinking on a full stomach slows absorption substantially-peak BAC can be 30–40% lower than the same drinks on an empty stomach. Food slows absorption; it does not eliminate it.
  • Rate of consumption: the liver clears about 0.015 BAC per hour-roughly one standard drink per hour. Drink faster than that and BAC rises. Drink slower and the liver keeps up.
  • Medications: opioids, benzodiazepines (Xanax, Valium), antihistamines (Benadryl), and many antidepressants amplify alcohol's effects. A customer on these meds at 0.05 BAC may behave like a customer at 0.10 BAC.
  • Tolerance vs. impairment: a regular heavy drinker may not LOOK impaired at a BAC that legally counts as impaired. Tolerance hides the visible signs-it does not change the BAC, the legal liability, or the reaction-time deficit.
Lesson

Tracking a real customer. Here's how the math plays out at the bar for one customer over 90 minutes:

Learning List

The setup

  • Customer: 160 lb male
  • Drinks: 3 × 12 oz domestic beer at 5% ABV (3 standard drinks)
  • Pace: one drink every 15 minutes between 8:00 and 8:45 PM
  • Liver clearance: about 0.015 BAC per hour (~1 standard drink/hour)
  • Consumption rate: 4× the liver's clearance rate
Learning List

Timeline of impairment

  • 9:00 PM - BAC approximately 0.07, just under Montana's 0.08 per-se DUI limit
  • 9:00–9:30 PM - BAC keeps climbing because alcohol he already drank is still absorbing
  • 9:30 PM - BAC likely above 0.08, even if he stops drinking now
  • 9:45 PM - clear impairment if you served a 4th drink at 9:00 PM
Note

The trap. A customer who reads as "still legal" right now may cross the line in the next 20–30 minutes from alcohol they already drank. Don't time service decisions on the current pour. Time them on where the BAC will be when the customer leaves and gets behind a wheel.

Lesson

BAC translates to behavior in roughly predictable bands. You don't need to estimate exact BAC-you need to recognize the band a customer is in and adjust service accordingly. The bands below are based on NHTSA driving-impairment research and are useful for service decisions, not for diagnosis.

Learning List

BAC bands and what you'll see

  • 0.02–0.04 (one to two drinks). Mild relaxation, slight mood lift. Most people show no obvious outward signs.
  • 0.05–0.07 (two to three drinks). Lower inhibitions, reduced judgment, slightly slowed reaction time. Customers may laugh louder, talk more, get more familiar. Driving is impaired; a Montana CDL holder is over the per-se limit at 0.04, and an under-21 driver is over at 0.02.
  • 0.08–0.12 (the per-se DUI band). Slurred speech, slowed reflexes, impaired coordination, balance issues, loss of fine motor control. This is the legal-impairment line for adults driving in Montana under MCA 61-8-1002.
  • 0.13–0.20. Visible drunkenness. Stumbling, dropping things, bloodshot or glassy eyes, loud or argumentative behavior, possible nausea. This person should not have been served the last drink. Continued service exposes the establishment to dram shop liability under MCA 27-1-710.
  • 0.20+. Risk of blackout, loss of consciousness, vomiting. Above 0.30, medical-emergency risk becomes real (alcohol poisoning, respiratory depression). Call 911-do not just send them home.
Note

Tolerance hides the warning signs but not the BAC. A regular drinker may show none of the 0.13–0.20 signs at a BAC of 0.18. Their breath, slurring, and stumbling are masked by tolerance. Their judgment, reaction time, and impairment are still compromised at the same BAC. Tolerance can hide visible cues, but it does not reduce impairment or make service safer. For service decisions, do not rely on "they look fine" if drink count, pace, or behavior suggests impairment. (Civil dram-shop liability under MCA 27-1-710 depends on the statutory triggers - including evidence of visible intoxication at the time of service - covered in Module 6.)

Scenario Practice

Scenario 1: "I had dinner first." A 130 lb woman comes to the bar at 7:45 PM. She mentions she had a steak dinner at 6:30 PM and has not had anything to drink yet tonight. Over the next 90 minutes she has three margaritas (your bar's standard pour: 2 oz tequila + 1 oz triple sec, so each margarita is ~1.7 standard drinks).

At 9:15 PM she orders a fourth.

Scenario Practice

Scenario 2: "He's a regular, he's fine." A 220 lb man you've served twice a week for three years comes in at 6:00 PM. He drinks four 16 oz craft lagers at 7.6% ABV (so each pint is about 1.9 standard drinks) over 2.5 hours. He doesn't slur.

He's not stumbling. He's having a calm conversation with his friend. He asks for a fifth.

Lesson

All of this physiology rolls up into one practical rule: you cannot serve based on drink count alone. A 240 lb regular drinker who has had four beers in two hours may be fine. A 110 lb first-timer who has had two cocktails on an empty stomach may be visibly intoxicated. You watch the person, not the receipt.

Lesson

In practice, the working tools for monitoring a customer are: count standard drinks (not menu items), track time on premises, watch for behavioral cues from the BAC bands above, and pay attention to context-has this person eaten, are they on medications, did they show up already affected? When in doubt, slow the service. The goal is not always to refuse the next drink; the goal is to keep the customer at a BAC that is safe for them and legal for you to serve.

Note

The slowing-service playbook. These are not refusals-they are how seasoned bartenders keep customers and licenses safe at the same time: • Suggest a water back with the next drink. • Suggest food: "the kitchen still has wings, want me to grab you an order?" • Slow the pour: take a longer route back to the bar, restock, take 5 extra minutes between rounds. • Switch to lower-ABV options: "how about a Coors Banquet? Great session beer." • Engage in conversation: a chatty bartender is a slower-pour bartender. • If the table is having a serious moment, refill water glasses and suggest the appetizer menu.

Lesson

Why slow service is also better business. Beyond the legal floor, the licensee's bottom line depends on customers coming back. A patron who is over-served on Tuesday and wakes up sick on Wednesday is unlikely to return; a patron who was served thoughtfully and left at a manageable BAC is the regular who walks back in next Friday. In practice, bars with strong service-pacing cultures tend to retain customers longer and earn more per visit from food and non-alcohol items than bars that simply push drinks.

The slowing-service playbook in the previous chunk is not a workaround for a refusal; it is a posture that lengthens the average visit, increases food attach, and reduces the catastrophic-event risk that ends licensee careers. Treating pacing as a culture rather than a checklist item helps a licensee on three fronts at once: better customer retention, lower insurance loss exposure, and a lower chance of a catastrophic over-service event.

Law

Why this matters (MCA 16-4-1006(1)(a)). Montana requires this course to teach how alcohol affects the human body. The point isn't biology trivia - it's so you can connect what you *see* in a customer to what's happening to their judgment and coordination, and act before someone gets hurt.

Learning List

What rising BAC looks like - and what you'll observe

  • 0.02–0.03 - Mild relaxation, slight mood lift. Little obvious change; most people look normal.
  • 0.05–0.06 - Lowered inhibition, louder, more talkative, slower judgment and fine-motor control. This is the practical caution zone for service.
  • 0.08 - Montana's per-se DUI limit for adult drivers (MCA 61-8-1002). Clear impairment: slurred speech, slowed reactions, poorer balance.
  • 0.10–0.15 - Obvious intoxication: staggering, slurring, poor coordination. At this point you must refuse service (MCA 16-3-301(4)(b)).
  • 0.20+ - Confusion, risk of blackout, possible vomiting. The person needs water, monitoring, and a safe ride - not another drink.
  • 0.30+ - Risk of unconsciousness and alcohol poisoning. This is a medical emergency - call 911.
Lesson
  • **Demonstration
  • why 'the same drinks' hit two people differently.** Picture two customers who each have three drinks in an hour. A 150-pound person on an empty stomach can reach roughly 0.08–0.09
  • legally impaired
  • while a 200-pound person who just ate a full meal might sit nearer 0.05. Same drinks, very different BAC. The factors that move the number: body size and composition, biological sex, an empty vs. full stomach, how fast they drink, carbonation (bubbles speed absorption), and medications. You can't measure BAC at the bar, so you watch behavior
  • but understanding these factors explains why you can never assume 'they only had three.'
Lesson

Demonstration - what one 'standard drink' really is (ARM 42.13.907(1)(a)(i)). One standard drink = 12 oz of 5% beer = 5 oz of 12% wine = 1.5 oz of 40% (80-proof) spirits. Each delivers about the same alcohol. But a heavy-handed pour, a 16-oz craft beer at 8%, or a double cocktail can each be 1.5–2 standard drinks.

So a customer who tells you they 'only had three' may have actually consumed five standard drinks' worth. This is the link between *what you pour* and *the BAC numbers above* - measured pours are a frontline tool for keeping customers on the safe side of the line.

Scenario Practice

Demonstration check. A customer has had what you served as 'three' drinks over about an hour - but two were 16-oz craft IPAs at 8%. He's now talking loudly, repeating himself, and fumbling his card.
