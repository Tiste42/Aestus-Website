MCA 61-8-1002 and Implied Consent

Lesson

Montana, like every state, has per-se BAC limits — numbers that establish a per-se alcohol or drug driving offense without separate proof of impairment. Per-se literally means "by itself": the number is the offense. Below those limits, a driver can still be charged with DUI based on observed impairment, but the per-se thresholds are the absolute lines.

Learning List

Per-se thresholds (MCA 61-8-1002)

  • 0.08 BAC for noncommercial drivers age 21+ — the standard adult driver
  • 0.04 BAC for commercial drivers (CDL holders) — half the standard limit
  • 0.02 BAC for drivers under 21 — Montana's zero-tolerance line
  • 5 ng/mL Delta-9 THC in blood — the separate per-se limit for cannabis
Law

MCA 61-8-1002 (summary): Montana's per-se DUI thresholds are codified in subsection (1) with separate subdivisions for each driver class — 0.08 alcohol concentration for non-commercial 21+ drivers, 0.04 for commercial drivers, 0.02 for drivers under 21, and 5 ng/mL Delta-9 THC (excluding inactive metabolites) for any driver. Meeting any threshold establishes per-se DUI without separate proof of impairment.

Note

Drink-count translation for the standard driver. A 160 lb adult typically reaches: • 0.02 BAC at about 1 standard drink in an hour • 0.04 BAC at about 2 drinks in an hour • 0.08 BAC at about 4 drinks in an hour • 0.16 BAC (aggravated DUI line) at about 8 drinks in 2 hours Body weight matters. A 110 lb adult hits each band on roughly half the drinks. A 220 lb adult on about 30% more.

Note

Aggravated DUI is a separate, harsher charge. It applies when: • The driver's BAC is 0.16 or higher, OR • The driver refused a chemical test AND has a prior DUI in the past 10 years Aggravated DUI carries longer mandatory jail and higher fines than a regular first-offense DUI. A bar that serves a customer past 0.16 and into aggravated-DUI territory faces stronger plaintiff arguments in any later dram-shop suit.

Lesson

When a person drives in Montana, they are deemed by law to have consented to a chemical test (breath, blood, or urine) on lawful arrest for DUI. The driver doesn't have to actually agree at the moment — the consent is built into the act of driving. Refusing the test triggers an automatic license suspension, separate from the DUI itself.

Learning List

Refusal penalties

  • 1st refusal: 6-month license suspension
  • 2nd refusal within 5 years: 1-year suspension
  • The refusal itself is admissible in court as evidence of consciousness of guilt
  • Refusal does NOT prevent the DUI charge — police can get a warrant for a forced blood draw under existing case law
Note

Why this matters at the bar. A regular customer who's had four drinks may believe they can "just refuse the breathalyzer and beat it." That's a misconception worth correcting — politely, when relevant — because: • Refusal still gets them a 6-month license suspension automatically • The refusal is admissible at trial against them • Police can get a warrant for a blood draw anyway • The refusal stacks with any aggravated-DUI exposure they already have • A $300 administrative fee is owed regardless of the criminal outcome The "refuse the test and walk" myth is one of the most common reasons impaired customers convince themselves it's safe to drive home.

Note

CDL holders face enhanced refusal penalties. A commercial driver who refuses a chemical test faces a 1-year disqualification for a first refusal and a lifetime disqualification for a second refusal (with possible reinstatement after 10 years under federal rules). This applies to ANY vehicle they're driving at the time of the stop, not just a commercial vehicle. A truck driver who refuses a breath test on the way home from your bar in his personal car loses his livelihood, not just his weekend.

If you have CDL regulars at your bar, this is worth knowing — most of them already know the rule and treat it as the floor, not the ceiling, of their personal cut-off.

Scenario Practice

Scenario: The refuser. A 38-year-old regular has had five drinks over 3 hours. As he's leaving, he tells you: "If they stop me, I'll just refuse the breathalyzer. Worst case I get a ride from a tow truck." He's a CDL holder — drives a propane truck for a living.

Learning List

Per offense

  • 1st offense: 24 hours to 6 months jail; $600–$1,000 fine; 6-month license suspension; mandatory ACT (Assessment, Course, Treatment) program. (Enhanced to 48 hours to 1 year jail and $1,200–$2,000 fine if a passenger under 16 was in the vehicle.)
  • 2nd offense: 7 days–1 year jail; $1,200–$2,000 fine; 6-month suspension; ignition interlock device (IID) required upon reinstatement
  • 3rd offense: 30 days–1 year jail; $2,500–$5,000 fine; 1-year suspension; IID required
  • 4th offense: FELONY. No statutory time limit on counting priors, so every prior DUI conviction still counts. 13 months to 2 years custody plus a consecutive 5-year prison term, or up to 5 years in a treatment-court program; $5,000-$10,000 fine; long-term IID; loss of certain civil rights during sentence
Note

The 4th-offense lookback. Some states only count prior DUIs from a 5- or 10-year window. Montana's felony-DUI statute sets no statutory time limit, so every prior DUI conviction on a person's record still counts toward the 4th-offense felony. A first DUI at 22 and a fourth at 55 still triggers the felony.

There is no "clean slate" period.

Note

Why the server should know this. When a customer at the bar is bragging about driving home or about a prior DUI, you now know what the next charge would cost them. That helps you offer a ride or call their friend with confidence: this is real money, real jail, and a felony on the fourth strike. It's also a credibility check — if a customer tells you "I've been pulled over six times and never gotten DUI" and then asks for one more, you have a clearer sense of what's actually at stake for them.

Lesson

You're not a cop and you don't have to estimate BACs. But you have huge influence at the moment a customer might choose to drive. Most servers underestimate how much a friendly intervention works.

The vast majority of customers — even ones who pushed back at the cut-off — accept a soft ride offer once they're walking toward the door.

Learning List

Server toolkit at the door

  • Offer a rideshare on the house. Many establishments comp $25 for a one-time Lyft or Uber. The cost is one drink; the savings (legal exposure + a customer's life) is everything.
  • Call a friend or family member listed in the customer's phone, with their consent. "Mom" or "home" is often listed.
  • Hold their car keys (with consent) while they sober up. Some bars have a designated key-hold board behind the bar.
  • Walk them to a cab line if there is one nearby and they refuse a rideshare.
  • Document the intervention in the incident log — covers the licensee under MCA 27-1-710 if there's ever a question about whether the bar tried to prevent driving.
  • Don't tackle them or take keys without consent. That's a different legal problem (false imprisonment, conversion). Persuasion and a comp ride is the lane.
Note

Phrasing that works. Customers respond to specifics, not generalities: • "Let me grab you a Lyft, on us. Five minutes." • "Mind if I call your wife? Just a heads-up so she's expecting you." • "You can leave the car here tonight. We open at 11; come back tomorrow." • "Coffee while you wait for the ride? Kitchen's still got fries." Generic "are you okay to drive?" gets "yeah I'm fine" 90% of the time.

Specific, no-friction offers get a yes.

Scenario Practice

Scenario 1: "I've done this drive a thousand times." A 220 lb regular has had four 16 oz craft pints (about 7 standard drinks) over 2 hours. He's slurring slightly and grabbing the bar to stand. He says, "I live three blocks away, I've done this drive a thousand times," and starts pulling out keys.

Scenario Practice

Scenario 2: The CDL holder. A semi-truck driver in a fluorescent vest comes in for happy hour after his shift. He's clearly off-duty. He orders one beer, drinks it slowly, then pays.

Lesson

Montana's implied-consent rule under MCA 61-8-1016 is one of the strongest tools on the law-enforcement side of the DUI framework. By operating a motor vehicle on a Montana road, a driver is deemed to have consented in advance to a blood, breath, or oral-fluid test if a peace officer has probable cause to believe the driver is impaired. Refusing the test triggers an administrative license suspension under MCA 61-8-1032, completely separate from any criminal DUI conviction. For a first refusal, the suspension is 6 months with no possibility of a restricted probationary license.

For a second-or-subsequent refusal within 5 years, the suspension is 1 year, again with no restricted-license option. Every refused implied-consent test also carries a $300 administrative fee payable to the department before the license can be reinstated. Commercial driver's license holders carry an even sharper consequence: a first refusal triggers a 1-year CDL disqualification under federal rules; a subsequent refusal triggers lifetime CDL disqualification. The server-side takeaway is straightforward — a customer you over-serve who then refuses a breath test the same night is looking at the criminal DUI charge AND a 6-month or 1-year administrative suspension AND a $300 fee, none of which depend on the prosecutor proving impairment in court.

Note

Aggravated DUI at 0.16 — the higher-tier threshold under MCA 61-8-1001. Montana defines 'aggravated driving under the influence' to include a per-se threshold of 0.16 BAC (twice the standard 0.08 per-se DUI level under MCA 61-8-1002). A driver charged at or above 0.16 faces enhanced penalties at every offense level: longer minimum jail terms, higher fines, longer ignition-interlock requirements, and (for repeat offenders) a faster path to the 4th-offense felony framework under MCA 61-8-1008. The aggravated-DUI threshold is also a useful number to know on the service side: a 180-lb adult male reaches 0.16 BAC after roughly 8 standard drinks in an hour on an empty stomach; a 130-lb adult female reaches it after roughly 6.

When you've watched a customer order their fifth or sixth round in a short window, the question on the table is not 'will they fail a breath test' but 'will they fail it at the aggravated level.' That's the math behind 'cut them off two rounds earlier than feels comfortable.' The 0.16 threshold also matters because it shifts the criminal-procedure picture: an aggravated DUI in Montana is one of the small set of misdemeanors where the prosecution often does not negotiate the charge down, and the mandatory minimum jail term on a first aggravated conviction is materially longer than a standard-tier first DUI. From the licensee's perspective, an aggravated-DUI defendant who was served at your bar is also the kind of defendant whose attorney will work backward from the breath-test result to the last bartender to pour — meaning the dram-shop notice under MCA 27-1-710(8) is more likely to arrive within the 180-day window when the underlying criminal case is aggravated than when it is a standard-tier first offense. For Montana servers, that converts directly into a service rule: when the table's drink count and timeline are tracking toward 0.16, the cost-benefit of cutting off two rounds early is overwhelmingly in favor of the cut.
